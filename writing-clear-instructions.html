Writing clear instructions on mbta.com

Content on mbta.com helps guide transit riders through their travel. Here's how I write instructions, knowing that most people scan and skim webpages for the info they need.

Check for an email confirmation

Working with a first draft from stakeholders, I helped write a more informative confirmation message for mbta.com's customer support form.

Previous copy:

Thank you for your feedback. Your comments have been sent to our Customer Support team.

Now:

Thank you for submitting your feedback. Our Customer Support team will review your comments soon.
If you provided your contact info, check your email for a confirmation with an incident number. If you don't receive a confirmation within 10 minutes, try submitting your feedback again or call us at xxx-xxx-xxxx.

Writing methods:

  • Recall exact terminology from the form [contact info]. This helps riders clearly determine if the instruction applies to them.
  • Use verbs like check, try, and call. This gives riders explicit and active steps they can follow.
  • Write precise time info [within 10 minutes]. This gives riders reassurance and a clear threshold for action.

Find the right ski train

Live webpage: Wachusett Mountain

I worked with stakeholders to write more detailed information about ski trains for the 2024 – 25 season.

Previous copy:

Each train will feature a specifically modified coach equipped with racks for ski and snowboard equipment.

Updated for the 2024 – 25 season:

The following Fitchburg Line trains will feature a specifically modified coach equipped with racks for ski and snowboard equipment:
  • Saturdays: Train 1403 to Wachusett and Train 1412 to North Station
  • Sundays: Train 2403 to Wachusett and Train 2412 to North Station

Writing methods:

  • Use bulleted lists. This makes important info stand out visually.
  • Frontload date/time info [Saturdays, Sundays]. This helps riders narrow down which point is relevant to them.
  • Write precise location info [to Wachusett, to North Station]. This helps riders know which direction to look for on a timetable.

Check back for opportunities to get involved

Live webpage: Durante Remediation and Wetland Enhancement

In an original content draft for this webpage, there was a statement telling riders that we'd update the page with more information on public meetings and project materials as it came up.

I worked with stakeholders to write a more active sentence that encourages riders' participation and frames things from a rider's perspective.

Please check back for upcoming opportunities to get involved, including:
  • An environmental justice virtual public meeting with the project team
  • Links to view the EENF and Rollover EIR
  • Ways to send your feedback about the EENF and Rollover EIR to the project team

Writing methods:

  • Use verbs in the imperative mood. This gives riders a concrete action to take.
  • Frame things from the riders' perspective: opportunities to get involved. This tells riders why they should care.
  • Get specific about benefits to the riders: links, ways to send feedback. This helps riders learn exactly what's in it for them.

[style guide] Capitalize after colons

In our content team's style guide, I updated a rule with examples to help content strategists know when to capitalize after colons.

My first draft:

When using a colon in body copy, capitalize the first word after the colon if it begins a full sentence (ex. Note: The new fare vending machines will be installed in spring). For project stats, we also capitalize the first word after the colon (ex. Status: In progress).

After a teammate's revision, with much better formatting:

When using a colon in body copy, capitalize the first word after the colon if it begins a full sentence. For example: "Note: The new fare vending machines will be installed in spring." For the status on project pages, we also capitalize the first word after the colon. For example: "Status: In progress."

Writing methods:

  • Use a "when A, do B" or "for A, do B" format. This gives situational context upfront.
  • Use examples to clearly illustrate the rule. This helps the team identify similar content to evaluate when to apply the rule (or not).
