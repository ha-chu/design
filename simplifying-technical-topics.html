Writing simply about technical topics on mbta.com

Here's how I simplify writing about technical topics on mbta.com, so more transit riders can learn about transit improvement projects at the T.

Light rail vehicle features

-> Public Viewing | Green Line Type 10 Vehicle Mock-Up (In-Person)

I worked with Vehicle Engineering to describe the features of a new light rail vehicle model, the Type 10, in plainer terms.

Original content reference:

  • Larger visual messaging signs for increased visibility of visual stop announcements
  • Digital passenger information screens to provide riders with real-time service information, including information on service disruptions.

Edited for mbta.com:

  • Dynamic digital signs for visual stop announcements
  • Digital signs that provide riders with real-time information on service and disruptions

Writing methods:

  1. Concrete, concise, and familiar terms: digital sign
  2. Plainer and fewer words: increased visibility
  3. Less repetition: service information, including information on service and disruptions

For more information, you can view the project webpage I later published about Type 10s.

Construction and procurement info

-> Transit-Oriented Development at Alewife

I worked with Transit-Oriented Development and Public Engagement teams to turn construction and procurement language into something more colloquial.

Language used in procurement docs:

To accomplish these goals, the MBTA will enter into a Pre-Development Agreement with its chosen JD partner to maximize public and private strengths of each party, and to create a development master plan that is realistic and viable.

First draft for mbta.com:

We're currently selecting a joint development partner to help create a project plan that's feasible and informed by community needs.

Final adaptation for mbta.com:

We're in the process of selecting a joint development partner. We'll collaborate with them on a project plan that's practical and informed by the needs of the community.

Writing methods:

  1. Pronouns and contractions to create familiarity and make reading more natural: we're, we'll
  2. Plain language: help, collaborate, project plan
  3. Break long sentences up
